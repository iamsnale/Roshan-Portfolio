Background

About Me

I'm a high school student at Tracy High School driven by an insatiable curiosity about the human brain and its potential intersection with technology. My journey into neuroscience began not in a classroom, but from a simple question: what makes us who we are?

The Academic Journey

My academic path has been shaped by a relentless pursuit of understanding the brain. From earning an A in Biology Honors to diving deep into AP Psychology and AP Seminar, each course has been a stepping stone toward my goal of becoming a neuroscience researcher. I've complemented my traditional coursework with a Harvard Online Python course, recognizing that modern neuroscience research demands strong computational skills.

The turning point came during UC Berkeley's ATDP program, where I studied Cognitive Neuroscience. Walking through UCSF's lab facilities and witnessing real scientists at work transformed my abstract interest into concrete determination. These weren't just researchers—they were people using their knowledge to address real medical questions and help real patients.

"Since I was little, I've been drawn to science, not just for the subject, but for what it stands for—exploring what is yet to be known."

Research & Innovation

Currently, I'm developing a science fair project on Brain-Computer Interfaces, exploring how technology can detect and respond to brain activity. This project represents the convergence of my interests: understanding neural signals, implementing machine learning algorithms, and creating practical applications that could one day help people with neurological conditions.

My fascination extends to neurodegenerative disorders like Alzheimer's and Multiple Sclerosis. These conditions drastically alter millions of lives, yet countless questions remain about their mechanisms and treatments. I'm particularly interested in how stem cell treatments and other therapeutic approaches might offer hope to those affected.

Beyond the Lab

Science doesn't exist in isolation for me. As a member of the National Honor Society and volunteer at Living Grace Assisted Living, I've learned to communicate complex concepts and see firsthand how neurological conditions impact real people and their families. My involvement in Speech and Debate has sharpened my ability to articulate scientific ideas clearly—a crucial skill for any researcher.

When I'm not immersed in research or coursework, I find balance through creative pursuits like sketching and baking, and physical activity through swimming. These hobbies remind me that the brain we study is also the source of creativity, discipline, and joy.

My goal is to become a researcher in cognitive neuroscience, focusing on neurodegenerative and neurological disorders. I want to go beyond studying science and use that knowledge to support those affected by conditions like Alzheimer's through innovative treatments.

Focus Areas

Research Interests

My research interests span multiple domains of neuroscience, from understanding the molecular basis of brain function to developing practical applications in brain-computer interfaces.

Brain-Computer Interfaces

Exploring how technology can detect and respond to brain activity. Currently developing a science fair project focusing on optimizing BCIs to detect motor commands and understanding how biological systems can interface with technological systems.

Neurodegenerative Disorders

Deep interest in understanding conditions like Alzheimer's and Multiple Sclerosis. Fascinated by how the brain can break down and committed to working toward solutions through means like stem cell treatments and other therapeutic approaches.

Cognitive Neuroscience

Passionate about understanding how the brain drives who we are. Interested in neural mechanisms of cognition, consciousness, and how cognitive processes can be studied and potentially enhanced or restored in neurological conditions.

Molecular Neuroscience

Eager to explore the molecular basis of neurological function and dysfunction. Interested in learning about neural signaling, synaptic mechanisms, and how molecular approaches can contribute to understanding and treating brain disorders.
